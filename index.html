Part 1

The Question

Read the question carefully. Before writing, you need to understand exactly what it demands.

“Museums are the rightful guardians of human remains.”

Evaluate this statement with reference to the ethical debates surrounding the study, display, and repatriation of ancient human remains. 20 Marks · 40–45 minutes · 600–800 words
Part 2

Deconstructing the Question

Work through each step. Write down your own answers first, then reveal to check.

Step 1

Identify the Command Verb

What is the key verb telling you what to do? Write it down.

The command verb is EVALUATE

What does “evaluate” require? What mistakes should you avoid?

Weigh up arguments on both sides — avoid only arguing one side
Reach a clear, reasoned judgement — avoid listing arguments without a judgement
Consider strength and limitations of each position — avoid describing without explaining significance
Use case studies and legal frameworks as evidence — avoid claims without evidence
Step 2

Identify the Stimulus

What statement are you responding to? What does the verb require you to do with it?

The stimulus is “Museums are the rightful guardians of human remains.”
You are not asked whether this is true — you must evaluate it: examine when it might hold true, and when it clearly does not.
Step 3

Break Down the Key Words

Identify the key content words. For each: what does it mean, and what should you ask yourself?

“Rightful guardians”
Questions of moral legitimacy — not just who currently holds the remains
Who else could claim guardianship? Does possession equal right?
“Human remains”
Not merely objects — they carry cultural, spiritual, and scientific significance
How does ‘remains’ vs ‘ancestors’ change the ethical argument?
“Ethical debates”
Engage with moral reasoning, not just historical events
What ethical principles conflict? (knowledge vs dignity, rights vs research)
“Study, display, repatriation”
Three distinct issues — address all three
Do they raise different ethical questions? Which case studies apply?
Step 4

Plan Before You Write

Before writing, check you can tick off each of these.

Do I have a clear position? (Partial agree/disagree is fine — but be decisive)
Can I name at least two case studies? (e.g. Lake Mungo + Lindow Man)
Can I cite at least two legal frameworks with dates? (NAGPRA 1990, UNDRIP 2007, ICOM 2017)
Have I addressed study, display and repatriation — or at least two of the three?
Does my plan include: Introduction + 3 PEEL paragraphs + Conclusion?

Introduction

The assertion that museums are the rightful guardians of human remains presents a compelling but ultimately incomplete picture of a complex ethical debate. While museums have historically fulfilled vital roles in preserving and disseminating knowledge about ancient peoples, the growing recognition of Indigenous cultural rights — and the colonial origins of many collections — fundamentally challenges any simple claim to guardianship. A balanced evaluation must weigh the scientific value of study and display alongside the spiritual obligations, legal frameworks, and human dignity of descendant communities.

Paragraph 1 — The Case for Museums

Museums provide irreplaceable conditions for the preservation, study, and public education of human remains. The case of Lindow Man, an Iron Age bog body discovered in 1984 in Cheshire and now held at the British Museum, demonstrates how institutional custody can enable ongoing scientific inquiry. With no identifiable descendant community making claims for return, the British Museum has employed DNA analysis, isotope studies, and CT scanning to reveal significant information about Iron Age Britain, presenting remains within full cultural and scientific context for public education. This illustrates how, in the absence of direct descendant communities, museum custody serves a legitimate guardianship function — protecting remains from deterioration, enabling continued research, and fulfilling the educational mission central to the institution’s purpose. In this context, the museum’s claim to guardianship is defensible, as the interests of historical knowledge serve broad public benefit with no identifiable party harmed by the arrangement.

Paragraph 2 — Challenging the Claim

However, the colonial origins of many museum collections fundamentally undermine the moral legitimacy of institutions claiming the role of rightful guardian. The repatriation of Mungo Lady (LM1) and Mungo Man (LM3) to the Paakantji, Ngyiampaa, and Mutthi Mutthi peoples illustrates what is at stake when institutional custody overrides community rights. Removed from Country without consent, these remains were held for decades in storage before Aboriginal campaigns secured their return to NSW National Parks and Wildlife Service custody in 1992, followed by formal reburial in 2017. The Traditional Custodians’ declaration — “Our ancestor is home” — powerfully articulates what decades of institutional custody had denied: the spiritual obligation of living communities to care for their dead. This case exposes a critical distinction: the ability to preserve and study human remains does not confer the right to do so. When remains were removed without consent as part of broader colonial dispossession, any claim to “rightful” guardianship is morally untenable, regardless of the scientific value produced. Lake Mungo thus directly refutes the central premise of the statement — capability does not confer legitimacy, and the experiences of descendant communities cannot be subordinated to the interests of scientific knowledge.

Paragraph 3 — Legal & Ethical Frameworks

The evolution of international and national legal frameworks over the past half-century reflects a growing consensus that museums cannot assume automatic rights to the custody of human remains. The 1990 Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) legally compelled US museums and federal agencies to return Native American remains and sacred objects to descendant communities. The 2007 UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), Article 12, formally recognised the right of Indigenous peoples to the repatriation of their human remains and cultural property. The ICOM Code of Ethics (2017) further required museums to respond with sensitivity to repatriation requests, giving genuine weight to the interests of originating communities. These frameworks do not reject museums as institutions, nor deny the value of scientific inquiry. Rather, they qualify museum authority — establishing that guardianship must be exercised in partnership with, not in spite of, descendant communities. Collectively, they represent a paradigm shift: from museums as unquestioned custodians of the past to stakeholders whose claims must be weighed alongside — and sometimes yielded to — the rights of living peoples.

Conclusion

Ultimately, the assertion that museums are the rightful guardians of human remains is too absolute to withstand rigorous ethical scrutiny. Where no identifiable descendant community exists and display serves a genuine educational purpose, institutional custody may be defensible. However, where living communities maintain spiritual, cultural, and legal rights to their ancestors — particularly where removal was non-consensual and colonial in nature — the museum’s claim to rightful guardianship fails on both moral and legal grounds. A just approach demands ongoing dialogue guided by principles of consent, dignity, and cultural respect. The question is not simply who owns the past, but who has the right to determine how it is honoured.

Part 4

Feature Guide

What each feature does, where it appears, and how to use it in your own writing.

Position (Introduction)

What it does: States your responding judgement in the very first sentence. It signals your stance on the stimulus without saying “I agree” or “I disagree.”

“…presents a compelling but ultimately incomplete picture…”
Tip: Write your position as a single sentence that a reader could understand without the rest of the essay. If it’s vague, it’s not a position.
Roadmap (Introduction)

What it does: Previews the 2–3 areas your response will address. Shows the marker your essay is planned, not improvised.

“A balanced evaluation must weigh the scientific value… alongside the spiritual obligations, legal frameworks, and human dignity…”
Tip: Your roadmap should map directly to your body paragraphs. If your roadmap says three things, you need three paragraphs.
P — Point

What it does: One sentence stating the paragraph’s argument. No evidence — just commit to a claim.

“Museums provide irreplaceable conditions…”
“However, the colonial origins… fundamentally undermine the moral legitimacy…”
Tip: Write all your Points first. If they read like a mini-essay, your structure is solid.
E — Evidence

What it does: Specific case studies, dates, legal frameworks, or primary source quotations.

“Lindow Man… 1984… DNA analysis, isotope studies, CT scanning…”
“NAGPRA (1990)… UNDRIP Article 12 (2007)… ICOM (2017)…”
Tip: 2+ case studies and 2+ legal frameworks. Present chronologically to show evolution.
E — Explain

What it does: Interprets evidence. No new info — explains why it supports the Point.

“…the ability to preserve and study human remains does not confer the right to do so.”
Tip: After evidence, ask: “So what? Why does this matter?” That answer is your Explain.
L — Link

What it does: Returns to the question’s language and advances the argument. Not a restatement — a progression.

“…capability does not confer legitimacy…”
Tip: Use exact words from the question. Qualify your claim to set up the next paragraph.
Conditional Judgement (Conclusion)

What it does: Delivers your final position using “where… / however, where…” to show that the answer depends on context.

“Where no identifiable descendant community exists… institutional custody may be defensible. However, where living communities maintain… rights…”
Tip: A conditional judgement is stronger than an absolute one. It shows you can distinguish between situations.
Elevation (Conclusion)

What it does: Moves beyond the specific question to a broader ethical or historical reflection.

“The question is not simply who owns the past, but who has the right to determine how it is honoured.”
Tip: Your final sentence should make the reader think beyond the essay. Aim for one sentence of genuine insight.
Judgement Language

What it does: These are the words and phrases that transform description into evaluation.

“compelling but ultimately incomplete” · “fundamentally undermine” · “morally untenable” · “does not confer the right” · “paradigm shift” · “defensible”
Tip: Build a personal bank of evaluative phrases. Practise inserting them into topic sentences, explain sentences, and links.

Quick-Reference Writing Tips

Language Precision

  • “Moral legitimacy” beats “right”
  • “Fundamentally challenges” beats “goes against”
  • “Paradigm shift” captures scale better than “change”
  • “Colonial dispossession” shows broad historical understanding

Evaluative Moves

  • Capability ≠ right (the core distinction)
  • “Where… / however, where…” for conditional judgement
  • Qualify — don’t generalise. “In this context” beats “always”
  • End with broader ethical reflection, not a summary

Evidence Standards

  • Name case study, date, and location every time
  • Cite specific articles (e.g. “UNDRIP, Article 12”)
  • One primary source quote per paragraph maximum
  • Present frameworks chronologically to show change

Structure Essentials

  • Introduction: position + roadmap (never “I agree/disagree”)
  • Body: 3 × PEEL, sequenced to build argument
  • Conclusion: stimulus + conditional judgement + elevation
  • Judgement language in every paragraph, not just the conclusion
