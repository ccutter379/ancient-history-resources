The Passage of Time and Human Remains

Deconstruct the question, analyse a model answer paragraph by paragraph, and master the PEEL structure with evaluative language.

Learning Intentions & Success Criteria

What We’re Learning

Learning Intention Success Criteria
We are learning to plan and write an extended response that evaluates how the passage of time affects ethical obligations regarding human remains, using case studies and legal frameworks as evidence.
  • I can identify the command verb, stimulus, and key content words in an extended response question
  • I can explain what “evaluate” requires and how it differs from “describe” or “explain”
  • I can select case studies that directly address the TIME dimension of the stimulus
  • I can identify the PEEL structure (Point, Evidence, Explain, Link) within a body paragraph

  • I can identify judgement language throughout a response and explain why it is essential for evaluation
Part 1

The Question

Read the question carefully. Before writing, you need to understand exactly what it demands.

“The passage of time diminishes the ethical obligation to return ancient human remains to their communities of origin.”

Evaluate this statement with reference to relevant case studies and legal/ethical frameworks. 20 Marks · 40–45 minutes · 600–800 words
Part 2

Deconstructing the Question

Work through each step. Write down your own answers first, then reveal to check.

Step 1

Identify the Command Verb

What is the key verb telling you what to do? Write it down.

The command verb is EVALUATE

What does “evaluate” require? What mistakes should you avoid?

Weigh up arguments on both sides — avoid only arguing one side
Reach a clear, reasoned judgement — avoid listing arguments without a judgement
Consider strength and limitations of each position — avoid describing without explaining significance
Use case studies and legal frameworks as evidence — avoid claims without evidence
Step 2

Identify the Stimulus

What statement are you responding to? What does the verb require you to do with it?

The stimulus is “The passage of time diminishes the ethical obligation to return ancient human remains to their communities of origin.”
You are not asked whether this is true — you must evaluate it: examine when time might genuinely reduce obligation, and when it clearly does not.
Step 3

Break Down the Key Words

Identify the key content words. For each: what does it mean, and what should you ask yourself?

“Passage of time”
The central variable — does the AGE of remains change our moral obligations?
Are 2,000-year-old remains different from 40,000-year-old remains? Why or why not?
“Diminishes”
Not “eliminates” — claims obligation is reduced, not removed. This nuance matters.
Is there a point at which obligation genuinely weakens? Or does it persist regardless?
“Ethical obligation”
A moral duty — not just a legal one. What principles create this obligation?
Where does this obligation come from? Cultural connection? Human dignity? Consent?
“Communities of origin”
Assumes a community exists to return remains to. What happens when it doesn’t?
Does the argument change when there IS a living community versus when there isn’t?
Step 4

Plan Before You Write

Before writing, check you can tick off each of these.

Do I have a clear position? (Partial agree/disagree is fine — but be decisive)
Have I chosen case studies where TIME is specifically relevant to the argument?
Can I cite at least two legal frameworks? Do any of them include a time limit?
Am I arguing BOTH sides — one paragraph supporting the stimulus and at least one challenging it?
Does my plan include: Introduction + 3 PEEL paragraphs + Conclusion?

Paragraph 1 — When Time May Matter

There are circumstances in which the passage of time may legitimately reduce the obligation to return remains, particularly when no identifiable descendant community exists to receive them. The case of Lindow Man, an Iron Age bog body discovered in 1984 and held at the British Museum, demonstrates this. With no identifiable descendant community making claims for return, the Museum has employed DNA analysis, isotope studies, and CT scanning to reveal significant details about Iron Age Britain. This illustrates that when vast periods of time have severed any identifiable cultural connection, institutional custody serves a legitimate function — protecting remains from deterioration and enabling continued research for broad public benefit. In this limited context, the statement’s claim that time diminishes obligation is defensible. However, this argument collapses when applied to cases where living descendant communities exist.

I Do — modelling: This paragraph argues the side the writer DISAGREES with. Explain to students that this is deliberate: a Band 5/6 response considers both sides. Point out the word “may” in the Point — it concedes the argument carefully without fully committing. Ask students to underline the evaluative words: “may legitimately,” “serves a legitimate function,” “defensible,” “collapses.”

Paragraph 2 — Challenging the Claim

The repatriation of Mungo Man fundamentally challenges the claim that time diminishes ethical obligation, demonstrating that a 40,000-year passage of time did not weaken the moral duty owed to descendant communities. Removed from Country without consent, the remains of Mungo Man (LM3) — approximately 40,000 years old — were held in institutional custody for decades before the Paakantji, Ngyiampaa, and Mutthi Mutthi peoples secured their return to NSW National Parks and Wildlife Service custody in 1992, followed by formal reburial in 2017. The Traditional Custodians’ declaration — “Our ancestor is home” — powerfully articulates the enduring spiritual connection that institutional custody had denied. This case demonstrates that the passage of time, no matter how vast, does not extinguish the cultural and spiritual bonds between communities and their ancestors. The obligation to return remains persisted across 40,000 years precisely because the community’s connection was never broken — it was merely overridden by colonial dispossession. Lake Mungo thus directly refutes the stimulus — time did not diminish obligation, and the community’s persistent claim exposes the statement’s assumption as historically and morally flawed.

We Do — guided practice: Co-construct this paragraph with the class. Students draft Point sentences in pairs (30 seconds), then cold call 2–3 pairs to share. Collect Lake Mungo evidence details on the board: removed without consent, 40,000 years old, returned 1992, reburied 2017, “Our ancestor is home.” Students write their own Link sentence independently (60 seconds), then reveal to check.

Paragraph 3 — Legal & Ethical Frameworks

International legal frameworks further undermine the statement by establishing repatriation rights without any temporal limitation. The 1990 Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act (NAGPRA) legally compelled US museums and federal agencies to return Native American remains regardless of their age. The 2007 UN Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples (UNDRIP), Article 12, formally recognised the right of Indigenous peoples to the repatriation of human remains and cultural property — again, with no time restriction. The ICOM Code of Ethics further requires museums to respond with sensitivity to repatriation requests, acknowledging community interests over institutional claims. The absence of any time limit in these frameworks is significant: the international community has explicitly rejected the idea that the passage of time diminishes the obligation to return remains. These laws do not distinguish between remains that are centuries old and those that are millennia old. Collectively, these frameworks confirm that the legal and ethical consensus stands in direct opposition to the statement’s central claim — obligation endures, regardless of time.

You Do — independent practice: Students write this paragraph independently using the scaffolded worksheet. They can check the PEEL scaffold afterwards. If CFU checks show more than a third of the class struggling after the ‘We Do’ phase, consider modelling this paragraph before releasing students to independent work.

Conclusion

The conclusion brings together three key moves: returning to the stimulus, delivering a conditional judgement, and elevating to a broader reflection.

Conclusion

Ultimately, the assertion that time diminishes the ethical obligation to return human remains cannot withstand scrutiny when tested against the evidence. Where no living descendant community can be identified, as in the case of Lindow Man, the practical expression of obligation may change. However, where communities maintain ongoing spiritual and cultural connections — as the Lake Mungo repatriation powerfully demonstrates — time does not weaken the moral duty owed. The deeper question is not whether time erases obligation, but whether those in power are willing to honour it.

Teaching moment: Point out the conditional pattern: “Where [X]… However, where [Y]…” This structure shows nuance — the writer distinguishes between situations rather than giving an absolute answer. The final sentence (Elevation) reframes the entire debate from a question about time to a question about power. Ask students: “Does this sentence introduce new evidence?” (No — it reframes. That’s what elevation does.)
Part 4

Feature Guide

What each feature does, where it appears, and how to use it in your own writing.

Position (Introduction)

What it does: States your responding judgement in the very first sentence. It signals your stance on the stimulus without saying “I agree” or “I disagree.”

“…oversimplifies a deeply contested debate…”
Tip: Write your position as a single sentence that a reader could understand without the rest of the essay. If it’s vague, it’s not a position.
Roadmap (Introduction)

What it does: Previews the 2–3 areas your response will address. Shows the marker your essay is planned, not improvised.

“An evaluation of the Lake Mungo repatriation, the case of Lindow Man, and international legal frameworks…”
Tip: Your roadmap should map directly to your body paragraphs. If your roadmap names three things, you need three paragraphs.
P — Point

What it does: One sentence stating the paragraph’s argument. No evidence — just commit to a claim.

“There are circumstances in which the passage of time may legitimately reduce the obligation…”
“The repatriation of Mungo Man fundamentally challenges the claim…”
Tip: Write all your Points first. If they read like a mini-essay, your structure is solid.
E — Evidence

What it does: Specific case studies, dates, legal frameworks, or primary source quotations.

“Lindow Man… 1984… DNA analysis, isotope studies, CT scanning…”
“NAGPRA (1990)… UNDRIP Article 12 (2007)… ICOM Code of Ethics…”
Tip: Choose case studies that directly address the TIME dimension of the stimulus. Generic examples weaken evaluation.
E — Explain

What it does: Interprets evidence. No new info — explains why it supports the Point.

“…the passage of time, no matter how vast, does not extinguish the cultural and spiritual bonds…”
Tip: After evidence, ask: “So what? Why does this matter?” That answer is your Explain.
L — Link

What it does: Returns to the question’s language and advances the argument. Not a restatement — a progression.

“…this argument collapses when applied to cases where living descendant communities exist.”
Tip: Good Link sentences look forward. Set up the next paragraph by signalling where the argument goes next.
Conditional Judgement (Conclusion)

What it does: Delivers your final position using “where… / however, where…” to show that the answer depends on context.

“Where no living descendant community can be identified… the practical expression of obligation may change. However, where communities maintain ongoing… connections…”
Tip: A conditional judgement is stronger than an absolute one. It shows you can distinguish between situations.
Elevation (Conclusion)

What it does: Moves beyond the specific question to a broader ethical or historical reflection.

“The deeper question is not whether time erases obligation, but whether those in power are willing to honour it.”
Tip: Your final sentence should make the reader think beyond the essay. Aim for one sentence of genuine insight.
Judgement Language

What it does: These are the words and phrases that transform description into evaluation.

“oversimplifies” · “may legitimately” · “serves a legitimate function” · “defensible” · “collapses” · “fundamentally challenges” · “does not extinguish” · “directly refutes” · “cannot withstand scrutiny”
Tip: Evaluation must run through the whole paragraph, not just appear in the Link at the end. If your paragraph only describes and then evaluates in the last line, you’re narrating with a judgement tacked on.

Quick-Reference Writing Tips

Language Precision

  • “Oversimplifies” beats “is wrong”
  • “Fundamentally challenges” beats “goes against”
  • “Does not extinguish” shows permanence better than “doesn’t remove”
  • “Colonial dispossession” shows broad historical understanding

Evaluative Moves

  • Argue the side you disagree with FIRST — finish strong
  • “Where… / however, where…” for conditional judgement
  • Qualify — don’t generalise. “In this limited context” beats “always”
  • End with broader reflection, not a summary

Evidence Standards

  • Choose case studies where TIME is directly relevant to the argument
  • Name case study, date, and location every time
  • Cite specific articles (e.g. “UNDRIP, Article 12”)
  • Ask: do any legal frameworks include a time limit? (No — that’s significant)

Structure Essentials

  • Introduction: position + roadmap (never “I agree/disagree”)
  • Body: 3 × PEEL — supporting argument first, then challenges
  • Conclusion: stimulus + conditional judgement + elevation
  • Judgement language in every paragraph, not just the conclusion
